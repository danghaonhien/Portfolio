I joined Endue at a very early stage. In the period of 6 months, I has been tasked with designing a MVP for Endue Software, a web application focused on managing infusion centers. The application aims to simplify the infusion management process by providing a centralized platform for healthcare providers and staff, as well as patients receiving infusion treatments.

This case study will explore my approach to designing the platform, the challenges encountered during the design process, and the solutions implemented to address these challenges. It will also highlight the benefits of the platform to the infusion care center, patients, and healthcare professionals

Infusion care centers are highly specialized facilities that require skilled professionals, advanced equipment, and strict protocols to ensure patient safety. This means we have to spend a great amount of research time to gain insight from the industry.

Here are what we aim for:

  • Understanding the complex workflows of healthcare providers and designing software that integrates seamlessly with their existing processes.

  • Conducting user research to identify pain points and design solutions that are intuitive and easy to learn.

  • Prioritizing user experience (UX) design to create a visually appealing, intuitive, and efficient platform.

  • Iteratively testing and refining the software based on user feedback to continuously improve user’s usability.

My product manager Thi Nguyen, CEO Brian Brett and I spent a week planning MVP considerations and features, focusing on understanding their current workflow, pain points, and challenges from the early insights gathered from previous interviews.

Here are what we learn:

  • Administrative tasks consume up to 30% of provider's time, making a modern software platform essential for streamlining these processes.

  • Medication errors during infusion therapy occur in 10-33% of cases, highlighting the need for better technology and processes.

  • Outdated and complex internal system, making staff’s workflows significantly slower.

Identify the existing systems used for managing patient care, inventory, and billing, and the specific needs for integrating these systems with Endue.

Document review happens immediately after the referral intake is received. For this process, an administrator or intake personnel (such as an intake coordinator) typically ensures that they have the patient's demographics, insurance card, and other necessary information to process the order. Often, there are minor issues with the document set (such as a missing signature or blurry page) that must be addressed.

We are addressing two problems when doing Document Review:

  • Performing an intake-level review to ensure that we have all the relevant information for a patient.

  • Conducting a clinical-level review to ensure that clinical teams understand the order and have the necessary information to safely administer medication based on the patient's medical history (while also cross-referencing against payer guidelines).

Our Proposal:

  • Our proposal to solve this issue is to allow both intake and clinical personnel (as appropriate) to dynamically tag and leave comments around document issues that need to be resolved. This eliminates the need to scrub through emails or leave sticky notes, and ensures that no important details are left out.

Key Criteria:

  1. Efficient status tracking system.

  2. Identify documents issues.

  3. Dynamically tag and leave comments.

Benefits Investigation must be run at time of onboarding and before each appointment (7 days before, 3 days before, morning of, etc.) to ensure ongoing protection for the clinic and the patients. Our team spent a good amount back and forth with our advisors to have a clear understanding of this process.

There are three things going on here:

  • Insurance Details.

  • Verification.

  • Running the eligibility metrics.

Key Criteria:

  1. Inputting the patient’s insurance information.

  2. Confirming verification.

  3. Confirming eligibility metrics.

We designed several rounds of iterations and have them tested with Benefit Specialists to get their insights. What we learned is that they either (i) conduct benefits investigations manually, (ii) rely on third-party BI checks, or (iii) do a combination of both. This is a critical part of the workflow because if insurance is not active, the clinic runs a huge exposure risk. In such cases, the full cost of a $50k administered drug (as an example) will fall to the patient, and that claim will never be paid. From these information, we redesigned to address these problems.

We are addressing the challenge of managing the time-consuming prior authorization process, which often causes delays that directly impact patient care timeliness and experience. This process is also a gating issue for scheduling patients. Prior authorizations typically have an expiration date, which can be problematic for patients undergoing ongoing treatments. They also have a maximum number of units/visits allowed and are directly tied to how clinics secure payment for services.

Our Solution:

  • To address this issue, we propose to capture the prior authorization stages in a light-touch manner until approval is obtained. This will help keep prior authorization cycle times as short as possible. Once approval is obtained, users will fill out key fields such as expiration date, ICD-10 codes, CPT codes, and maximum number of units/visits. This will ensure compliance with prior authorization documentation.

Key Criteria:

  1. Submit for Approval.

  2. Track Status Stages

  3. Record and Update if Success

  4. Contact and Upload Supported Documents if Failed

Scheduling is one of the most important tasks in the app. If done right, it will help enhance the clinic efficiency for both patients and staffs.

We conducted interviews with a clinic scheduler, and she walked us through how their system is using. In our system, we need to schedule appointments for both new and existing patients, but this is dependent on several factors, including:

  • Having an active order for the patient

  • Having an active prior authorization (if necessary)

  • Confirming that the patient has active insurance

  • Being able to procure the medication (BnB or SP) before the patient's arrival

  • The patient's availability

  • The clinic's chair/staff availability

For a week, our team gathered to create diagrams in order to design an effective scheduling system.

We explored a couple of iterations to test out the logics.

We are addressing the problem of disorganized patient profiles. Users often lack full transparency on a patient's status and must click around to find the information they need. Having access to patient profiles is crucial in ensuring seamless communication and a positive patient experience. This includes being able to reconfirm prior authorizations, determine the co-pay programs a patient is enrolled in, review their medical history, and access their insurance information. Unfortunately, many of the advisors we spoke with reported having to search through patient profiles to confirm certain documents, communicate internally to confirm prior authorizations, or contact others via email or phone to obtain notes on a specific patient.

The infusion management process can be complex and time-consuming, involving multiple healthcare providers, patients, and medications. By providing a centralized platform for scheduling appointments, managing inventory, and monitoring patient progress, the MVP designed for Endue Software aimed to streamline this process and improve the overall patient experience.


The impact of this MVP on the healthcare industry could be significant. According to a survey by the National Infusion Center Association, over 3 million patients in the US receive infusion treatments each year, and this number is expected to continue to grow. This trend highlights the need for efficient and effective management of infusion centers, which the MVP designed for Endue Software aims to address.


The potential benefits of the MVP designed for Endue Software include improved patient outcomes, increased efficiency in infusion center management, and reduced costs for healthcare providers and patients. By streamlining the infusion management process, the application could reduce wait times for patients, minimize medication errors, and improve patient satisfaction.


Real-life statistics support the potential benefits of the MVP. For example, a study published in the Journal of Oncology Practice found that the use of electronic health records (EHRs) in cancer care was associated with a 38% reduction in medication errors. Additionally, a survey by the Advisory Board found that healthcare providers who use EHRs reported an average 25% reduction in medication errors.


However, there may also be challenges associated with the implementation of the MVP designed for Endue Software. One potential challenge could be resistance from healthcare providers and staff who are accustomed to using traditional methods for infusion center management. Training and support would be necessary to ensure a smooth transition to the new platform.

During my six months working as a product designer for Endue Software, I had the opportunity to design an MVP for a web application that focused on infusion center management. This experience has taught me a great deal about the design process, working with stakeholders, and designing for a specific target audience.


Firstly, I learned that a successful design process is rooted in collaboration and communication. It is important to work closely with stakeholders, including the development team and end-users, to gather feedback and iterate on the design. This approach ensures that the final product is tailored to the needs of the users and is both functional and user-friendly.


Secondly, I learned that designing for a specific target audience requires a deep understanding of their needs and pain points. In the case of infusion center management, it was important to understand the workflows and challenges faced by healthcare providers and staff, as well as patients receiving infusion treatments. This knowledge helped inform the design decisions and ensure that the application met the needs of all users.


Finally, I learned that designing an MVP requires a balance between functionality and simplicity. It is important to prioritize key features and ensure that the application is easy to use and understand, while still providing value to the users.
